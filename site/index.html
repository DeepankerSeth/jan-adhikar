Tear gas, heat, crowd crush, panic — quick first aid
  • Tear gas: move to fresh air. Rinse eyes with plain water for 10–15 minutes; remove contact lenses; do not rub. Do not use milk. Remove and bag contaminated clothing; wash skin with soap and water. (CDC and clinical guidance)
  • Heat: move to shade, sip water. Confusion, hot dry skin or fainting is an emergency — call 112 and cool the person with wet cloths and fanning. (WHO guidance)
  • Dense crowds: know your exits, stay near the edges, keep your arms in front of your chest; if you fall, curl up and protect your head.
  • Panic: breathe in and out slowly, counting to five. It usually passes in minutes.

Essential safeguards after arrest

Subject to constitutional exceptions, the arrested person must be told the grounds of arrest — the Supreme Court requires them in writing, in a language the person understands (2025). They have the right to consult and be defended by a lawyer of choice, to meet an advocate during interrogation (though not throughout it), and must be produced before the nearest magistrate within 24 hours excluding journey time. Ask for the arrest memo; immediate notice to the nominated person of both arrest and place of custody; and a medical examination that records injuries, with a report copy.

If police are investigating an offence punishable by up to seven years, a written notice to appear — not arrest — is the rule (BNSS s.35(3); Supreme Court, 2026). Preventive detention during prohibitory orders works differently: the person must still be told the reason, and detention beyond 24 hours needs separate legal authority.

A woman should not ordinarily be arrested after sunset and before sunrise, except in exceptional circumstances with a magistrate's permission (BNSS s.43(5)), and a woman's medical examination must involve a female practitioner. A minor cannot be kept in a police lockup or jail: they must go before the Juvenile Justice Board within 24 hours, a guardian must be informed, and free legal aid is automatic.

Human-rights complaint involving a public servant

For a violation or abetment by a public servant, or public-servant negligence in preventing one. NHRC is not emergency dispatch. It ordinarily screens out matters older than one year, sub judice, vague or anonymous complaints and service matters. Its actions are principally inquiries, recommendations, interim-relief recommendations or court approaches—not guaranteed compensation or prosecution.

Save the essentials before you need them.

Download emergency card
If mobile internet is shut down
  1. Shutdown orders usually stop mobile data while voice calls and person-to-person SMS keep working — but near a police action even calls can fail. Never make a phone call your only plan.
  2. Calling 112 needs no internet and no balance. The 112 phone app does need internet — do not rely on the app during a shutdown.
  3. Fix a meeting point and check-in times that work with no network at all, and keep numbers on paper.
Prepare before you go (2 minutes)
  1. Charge and back up your phone.
  2. Write key numbers on paper: 112, legal aid, one trusted person.
  3. Agree a meeting point and fixed check-in times with family or friends.
  4. Use a strong PIN; consider switching off fingerprint/face unlock and lock-screen message previews for the day.
  5. Check what auto-uploads from your phone (photos/cloud) before recording anything sensitive.
  6. Do not post live locations of yourself or others; share after leaving the area, minding faces and consent.
How offline use works
  1. Open this site once while connected; supported browsers will keep the essential page for offline use.
  2. Download or screenshot the emergency card and keep the numbers on paper.
  3. Agree on a meeting point, check-in time and one trusted contact before entering a crowd.
